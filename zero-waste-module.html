What is Zero-Waste?

🌍 Understanding the Zero-Waste Concept

Zero-waste is a philosophy and a practical approach that aims to eliminate waste by redesigning how we use resources — so that nothing ends up in a landfill, incinerator, or the ocean.

In Zamboanga City, we generate thousands of kilograms of waste every day. Much of it ends up in open dumpsites or our coastal waters — harming marine life, spreading disease, and polluting the air we breathe.

💡 Did you know?
The Philippines generates about 35,000 metric tons of solid waste daily. Zamboanga City's coastal communities are among the most affected by improper waste disposal.

Why Does It Matter in Zamboanga?

  • Protects our coastal ecosystems and fishing communities
  • Reduces health risks from open dumping and burning
  • Creates livelihood opportunities through upcycling and recycling
  • Helps Zamboanga City comply with RA 9003 (Ecological Solid Waste Management Act)
  • Supports ZGIH's mission of sustainable community development

Waste Segregation

🗑️ Why Segregate at Source?

Waste segregation means separating your waste into categories before it leaves your home. This is the single most important habit in a zero-waste community.

When waste is mixed together, recyclable materials become contaminated and must go to the landfill. When waste is segregated, each type can go to the right destination — composting, recycling, or proper disposal.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Many households throw everything into one bag. This contaminates compostable materials with chemicals and makes recyclables unsaleable.

The 3 Basic Categories

🍌

BIODEGRADABLE

Food scraps, fruit peels, leaves, paper, garden waste. These can be composted into organic fertilizer.

🥤

NON-BIODEGRADABLE

Plastics, metals, glass, rubber. These take hundreds of years to break down but many can be recycled or upcycled.

☠️

HAZARDOUS / SPECIAL

Batteries, paint, medicine, syringes, chemicals. These require special handling and must NEVER go in regular bins.

🇵🇭 Philippine Law: Under RA 9003, all LGUs are required to implement a solid waste management program including mandatory waste segregation. Zamboanga City enforces this through Executive Order KHYM 062-2025.

Practical Segregation Tips

  • Place at least 2 bins in your kitchen — one for biodegradable, one for recyclables
  • Rinse recyclables before placing them in the bin to avoid pests and odors
  • Keep a small sealed container for hazardous materials until collection day
  • Label your bins clearly — especially for children and household helpers
  • Never mix wet and dry waste

The 9-Bin Color-Coded System

🟩 Zamboanga City's Official System

Under Executive Order KHYM 062-2025, Zamboanga City adopted a 9-bin color-coded waste segregation system. Each color represents a specific waste type. Knowing these colors is essential for every household and barangay.

🟢
GREEN
Biodegradable
Food scraps, leaves, organic
🔵
BLUE
Recyclable Paper
Cardboard, newspapers, books
🟡
YELLOW
Recyclable Plastic
Bottles, sachets, containers
🔴
RED
Hazardous Waste
Batteries, chemicals, paint
⚫
BLACK
Residual Waste
Non-recyclable, soiled items
🩷
PINK
Sanitary / Biomedical
Diapers, masks, sanitary pads
🟠
ORANGE
Recyclable Metal
Cans, scrap metal, foil
🔷
VIOLET
Recyclable Glass
Bottles, jars, mirrors
🟣
PURPLE
E-Waste
Old phones, wires, appliances
📌 Memory Tip: Think of the bins by purpose — GREEN for nature, BLUE for paper, YELLOW for plastic, RED for danger, BLACK for the rest.

Common Sorting Mistakes

❌ Wrong

Placing styrofoam in the yellow (plastic) bin

✅ Right

Styrofoam goes in the black (residual) bin — it is not recyclable

❌ Wrong

Putting soiled food containers in the yellow bin

✅ Right

Rinse the container first, then place in yellow bin

Upcycling Basics

♻️ From Trash to Treasure

Upcycling is the process of transforming waste or useless materials into new products of better quality or environmental value. Unlike recycling (which breaks materials down), upcycling elevates the item into something more useful or beautiful.

♻️ Recycling

Breaking down material into raw form (melting plastic, pulping paper) then manufacturing new products. Requires industrial processing.

⬆️ Upcycling

Creating something new and valuable directly from waste without breaking it down. Can be done at home or in the community. Higher value output!

ZGIH Upcycling Projects You Can Try

🧴

Plastic Bottle Planters

Cut plastic bottles in half. Fill with soil. Grow herbs, vegetables, or flowers. String them together as a vertical garden.

👜

Woven Plastic Bags

Cut plastic sachets and candy wrappers into strips. Weave into durable, colorful bags, mats, or baskets — a ZGIH signature product!

🕯️

Tin Can Lanterns

Punch patterns into tin cans. Insert a candle or LED light. Beautiful decorative lanterns from food cans!

📦

Cardboard Furniture

Layer and glue corrugated cardboard to make sturdy stools, shelves, or storage boxes. Lightweight and surprisingly strong!

🛞

Tire Garden Pots

Old tires painted in bright colors become durable outdoor planters. Stack them as raised garden beds.

🪡

Fabric Scrap Rugs

Braid or weave old clothing scraps into colorful floor mats and rugs. Zero fabric waste!

💼 Livelihood Opportunity: ZGIH members sell upcycled products through the ZGIH Repurpose Store. You can turn your upcycling skills into income while helping the environment!

Community Action

🤝 Zero-Waste Starts with the Community

Individual habits matter — but real zero-waste change happens when whole communities act together. A barangay that practices proper segregation, composting, and upcycling creates a cleaner, healthier, and more prosperous neighborhood for everyone.

How to Start a Zero-Waste Barangay Program

1

Organize a Barangay Eco-Committee

Form a small team of motivated residents — ideally including youth, women, and senior members. This committee leads waste programs and monitors compliance.

2

Set Up Communal Segregation Points

Place color-coded bins in high-traffic areas — sari-sari stores, waiting sheds, school entrances. Label them clearly with pictures for non-readers.

3

Start a Community Compost Program

Collect biodegradable waste and compost it in a shared area. The resulting fertilizer can be used in community gardens or sold to local farmers.

4

Run Regular Clean-Up Drives

Organize monthly or quarterly clean-up days involving all households. Document and report your collected waste — ZGIH can help you track your impact!

5

Partner with ZGIH

ZGIH provides training, materials, and market access for barangays that want to start upcycling programs. Contact us to learn how to get started!
